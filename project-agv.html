Project P — 005

Autonomously
Guided Vehicle

A small line-following robot built to traverse a track, execute a three-point turn, and trace the route back — repeatedly and reliably, all inside tight material and dimensional constraints.

2021 Dublin City University Mechatronic Design
01

Overview

The brief: design and manufacture a small autonomous vehicle that follows a line, performs a three-point turn at the end of the track, and traces its route back — on repeat. The constraints made it interesting: limited materials, a strict dimensional envelope, and a defined parts budget.

The work split cleanly across three layers — mechanical design and manufacture for the chassis and drive train, electronic integration for sensors and motor drivers, and the control logic that turns a line of contrasting tape into a stable steering signal.

Line following is a deceptively simple problem. Off-the-shelf the algorithm fits on a page; tuned to a real robot with real motors and a real track, the details — sensor placement, threshold calibration, response curve — are where the project actually lives.

Year 2021
Context DCU coursework

02

System Breakdown

S — 01
Sensor Array

A line of contrast-detection sensors mounted at the front of the chassis. Sensor spacing and stand-off height were tuned against the track's line width — the geometry sets the resolution of the steering signal.

S — 02
Drive Train

Differential-drive chassis with motors driven through dedicated motor-driver electronics. Differential drive makes turning straightforward — adjust the speed differential between left and right and the robot arcs accordingly.

S — 03
Control Logic

A control loop converts the sensor array's readings into a target speed differential. The loop has to react fast enough to keep the line centred without oscillating off the track on every correction.

S — 04
Three-Point Turn Sequence

A scripted manoeuvre triggered at the end of the line: forward, reverse with steering, forward again — turning the robot 180° in a space smaller than its turning radius. The trick is detecting that the line has ended, not mistaking a gap for a turn cue.
