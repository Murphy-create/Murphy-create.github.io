Project P — 009

Fireman
Robot

A robot that climbs a 1-metre ladder carrying an open beaker of water without spilling — and races head-to-head against other student designs.

2022 Dublin City University Competitive Build
01

Overview

A competitive university project with one of the better engineering briefs I've encountered: design and build a robot that can scale a 1-metre vertical ladder while carrying an open beaker of water — without spilling a drop — and race it head-to-head against other student designs.

The brief is sharper than it sounds. You're working under strict constraints on materials, components, and manufacturing processes; the beaker is open, so any acceleration that throws water out costs you; and the ladder is vertical, so the robot has to fight gravity all the way up. Speed matters because it's a race — but speed without smoothness drains the beaker.

That trade-off — fast versus smooth, aggressive versus stable — is what the whole design problem reduces to. Every decision on drive train, grip mechanism, payload mounting, and control loop comes back to it.

02

Design Trade-Offs

T — 01
Speed vs Spillage

The race rewards speed; the open beaker punishes acceleration. The optimum isn't max speed — it's max-smooth speed. The whole climb profile has to be tuned to stay under the threshold where water sloshes out.

T — 02
Climbing Mechanism

A vertical ladder needs positive grip — the robot can't rely on friction alone, since gravity is always trying to pull it back down. The grip mechanism has to engage rungs reliably and release them quickly enough not to stall the climb.

T — 03
Payload Mounting

Where the beaker sits on the robot dictates how it experiences acceleration. A high mounting point sees more whip; a low mounting point keeps the centre of mass favourable; a gimballed mount decouples the beaker from the chassis but costs weight. Each option trades against the others.

T — 04
Working Within Constraints

The challenge of constrained-build projects is that you can't engineer your way out with a bigger budget — you have to engineer your way out with a better idea. Material, component, and process limits force creativity onto the design rather than the bill of materials.
