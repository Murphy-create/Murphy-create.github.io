Project P — 007

Hydrodynamic
Boat Project

Model a hull, derive the maths, print the boat, race it — and find out exactly how well the analytical prediction matches reality.

2021 Dublin City University Simulation & Verification
01

Overview

A first proper exercise in closing the loop between simulation and reality: design something analytically, predict how it will behave, then build it and see whether the prediction holds up.

The hull was modelled in SolidWorks. From the geometry I derived a velocity-time curve, the boat's tilt angle under load, and the predicted travel time over a fixed distance. The hull was then 3D-printed and raced against the analytical prediction in physical testing.

The lesson sat in the gap between the two: where the model held, where it didn't, and which assumptions were quietly doing the heavy lifting. An early version of the same loop I'd later run on far bigger projects.

Year 2021
Context DCU coursework
CAD SolidWorks
Manufacture 3D-printed hull
02

Method

M — 01
CAD Modelling

The hull was designed in SolidWorks with attention to displaced volume, wetted area, and the basic mass distribution that drives the boat's tilt under propulsion.

M — 02
Analytical Derivation

Derived a velocity-time curve from a force balance: propulsive force against drag. From that, integrated to predicted travel time. Also computed the tilt angle the boat would adopt under the propulsion load.

M — 03
Manufacture & Testing

3D-printed the hull and ran it under the same conditions assumed by the model. The race was the experiment — predicted travel time against measured travel time, with the difference telling the story.

M — 04
What This Project Taught

An early, honest encounter with the gap — and the bridge — between simulation and reality. Models are useful precisely because they're wrong in known ways; the engineering judgement is knowing which wrongness matters.
