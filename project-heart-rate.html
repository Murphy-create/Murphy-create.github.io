Project P — 006

Heart Rate
Monitor PoC

A photoplethysmography sensor built from an LDR, a green LED, and an Arduino — an honest first encounter with bio-signal processing.

2022 Personal Project Signal Processing
01

Overview

Photoplethysmography (PPG) is how the green light on a smartwatch reads your pulse. A light source illuminates the skin, a sensor measures the light reflected or transmitted back, and the tiny variation in absorption caused by each pulse of blood flow shows up as a periodic signal.

This project rebuilds that principle from first parts: a green LED illuminates the skin, an LDR senses the returning light, and an Arduino reads the LDR's voltage as a one-channel analogue signal. The raw signal is noisy; a 5-point moving-average filter smooths it just enough to expose the underlying pulse waveform.

The result was a clear, repeatable pulse trace — proof the principle works at hobby-scale parts cost. Attempts to extend the rig to SpO₂ measurement were inconclusive, but the build was a useful first lesson in the gap between a textbook signal and one you have to actually pull out of noise.

02

Build Breakdown

B — 01
Optical Front End

Green LED chosen because oxygenated blood absorbs green wavelengths strongly — the same reason commercial PPG sensors look green. The LDR sits adjacent to the LED, reading reflected light back through the skin.

B — 02
Analogue Signal Path

The LDR's resistance modulates with returning light intensity. A voltage divider converts that into an analogue voltage the Arduino's ADC can sample. The pulse signal rides on top of a much larger DC offset — extracting it cleanly is the core challenge.

B — 03
Moving Average Filter

A 5-point moving average smooths sample-to-sample noise without blurring the underlying pulse waveform. Short enough to preserve the heartbeat's shape, long enough to suppress the noise on top of it. A first practical lesson in trading filter length against signal fidelity.

B — 04
What Worked & What Didn't

Pulse detection worked — clean, repeatable peaks correlated with heartbeat. SpO₂ measurement attempts were inconclusive: getting two wavelengths and the calibration right is meaningfully harder than reading rate, and the parts on hand weren't up to it. An honest finding, not a failure — the kind that points to the next iteration.
